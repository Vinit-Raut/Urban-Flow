Solution

Smart water harvesting during floods integrates advanced technology with traditional water management systems to optimize the capture, storage, and utilization of excess rainwater in urban areas. It uses sensors, IoT devices, and data analytics to monitor rainfall, water levels, and drainage conditions in real time. When heavy rain is detected, automated systems redirect floodwaters to designated storage facilities such as underground tanks, retention basins, or urban wetlands. These facilities are designed to prevent overflow while maintaining water quality. Collected water can be filtered and reused for non-potable purposes, like irrigation or industrial processes, or redirected to recharge groundwater aquifers. Smart systems also predict potential flood zones and dynamically adjust water flows to mitigate flooding risks, creating a sustainable solution for urban water challenges.

Real-Time Monitoring and Data Collection

Smart water harvesting systems begin with an array of sensors installed throughout a city. These sensors measure parameters such as:

  • Rainfall intensity and duration.
  • Water levels in storm drains, rivers, and reservoirs.
  • Groundwater saturation levels.
  • Soil moisture content in permeable areas.
Soil moisture content in permeable areas.

Predictive Analytics and AI Integration

Using historical data, weather forecasts, and current sensor readings, advanced algorithms predict flood-prone areas and assess the volume of water that can be harvested. Artificial intelligence (AI) models simulate water flow patterns, enabling the system to identify optimal locations for water diversion and storage.

Dynamic Water Redirection

When the system detects a risk of flooding:

  • Automated gates or valves in stormwater systems are activated to redirect excess rainwater to designated water storage facilities.
  • These facilities could include underground tanks, retention ponds, or green infrastructure like bioswales and urban wetlands.
  • For cities near rivers or lakes, controlled release mechanisms prevent overflow and safely divert water to secondary holding areas.

Storage and Treatment

The harvested water is stored in facilities designed to prevent contamination, such as:

  • Underground reservoirs: Large tanks capable of storing millions of liters of water.
  • Retention basins: Surface-level ponds or lakes that temporarily hold water during heavy rains.
To ensure usability, the water is treated using filtration systems that remove debris, sediments, and potential pollutants. Advanced treatment facilities may also disinfect water, making it suitable for non-potable uses like irrigation or industrial cooling.

Groundwater Recharge

Excess water that cannot be immediately used is redirected to recharge aquifers. This involves channeling water through recharge wells or permeable infrastructure that allows it to percolate into the ground, replenishing depleted groundwater reserves.

Smart Distribution and Utilization

Collected water is distributed efficiently using smart networks. For example:

  • Sensors monitor the water levels in storage facilities to optimize usage and prevent overflow.
  • Stored water is used for urban greening projects, firefighting, street cleaning, or as a backup supply for municipal water systems during dry periods.
  • Some systems integrate with irrigation networks in parks and gardens, ensuring optimal water use.

Community and Ecosystem Benefits

Beyond flood control and water reuse, smart water harvesting supports:

  • Reduced strain on stormwater drainage systems, minimizing infrastructure damage.
  • Creation of green spaces (wetlands or retention ponds) that enhance urban biodiversity.
  • A resilient water supply system that buffers cities against droughts or water shortages.

User-Friendly Interfaces

City authorities and citizens can monitor the system’s performance via user-friendly dashboards. These platforms display real-time data on rainfall, water storage, and flood risk levels, empowering communities to respond proactively.
